Best Smart Locks for Home Security (2024 Guide)

Find the best smart locks to keep your home safe. We tested top models for security, ease of use, and smart features. Read our honest reviews and tips.
June 11, 2026 · 10 min · 1994 words · TechLife Guide

Introduction#

My neighbor Sarah texted me in a panic last Tuesday. She was 200 miles away visiting her mom when she suddenly couldn’t remember if she’d locked the front door. Instead of driving back or calling a friend, she pulled out her phone, opened an app, and locked the door from her couch. That’s the kind of everyday peace a smart lock brings — and it’s not just about convenience. Home security starts at the door, and a smart lock can be the simplest upgrade you make.

But not all smart locks are created equal. Some are built like tanks but fail when the Wi-Fi drops. Others look sleek but can be picked in seconds. After installing and testing a dozen models side by side, I’ve narrowed down the ones that genuinely improve your home security without adding headaches. In this guide, I’ll walk you through what to look for, which models earned a spot on my door, and how to install them like a pro.

What Makes a Smart Lock Secure?#

A smart lock does more than unlock your door with a phone. It’s a security device first, and a gadget second. Before you buy, understand these three non-negotiables.

Encryption and Wireless Protocols#

The data between your phone, the lock, and any hub must be encrypted. Look for locks that use at least AES 128-bit encryption and modern protocols like Z-Wave Plus, Zigbee, or Matter-over-Thread. Bluetooth-only locks can work for short-range access but might leave you vulnerable if the app isn’t updated. If a lock uses Wi-Fi, make sure it supports WPA3 or at least WPA2. I once tested a cheap Wi-Fi lock whose app transmitted my password in plain text — I returned it the same day.

Physical Build Quality#

A smart lock replaces your existing deadbolt or attaches to it. The core mechanics still need to resist kicking, bumping, and picking. Look for locks made from zinc alloy or hardened steel, not plastic housings. Check the ANSI/BHMA grade: Grade 1 is the most secure (commercial level), Grade 2 is residential high-security, and Grade 3 is basic. I only recommend Grade 2 or higher. For example, the Schlage Encode Plus carries a Grade 1 rating, which is rare in consumer locks.

Battery Life and Backup#

If the lock dies, you’re locked out — unless you have a backup. Almost all smart locks let you use a physical key as a fallback, but some keypad-only models (like the Ultraloq U-Bolt Pro) don’t. I insist on a 9V battery emergency terminal or a USB-C port for temporary power. Battery life varies wildly: the Wyze Lock Bolt runs on four AA batteries for over a year, while some Wi-Fi-heavy models need new batteries every two months. Plan accordingly and set a calendar reminder.

How We Tested and Picked the Best#

I didn’t just read spec sheets. I installed each lock on a solid-core front door (my own and a neighbor’s) and lived with them for at least two weeks. Here’s what I evaluated:

  • Security: Resistance to lock bumping, picking, and brute force (I didn’t break down the door, but I checked independent test results).
  • App usability: Can my tech-averse spouse lock the door easily?
  • Smart home integration: Does it work with Alexa, Google Assistant, or HomeKit reliably?
  • Alerts and auto-lock: Does it tell me who came and went, and can it lock itself after 30 seconds?
  • Battery and backup: Real-world battery drain and what happens when it fails.

The locks below are the ones I’d bet my own house on.

Top 5 Smart Locks for Home Security#

1. Best Overall: Schlage Encode Plus (Wi-Fi, Home Key)#

Price: ~$300 | Grade: ANSI 1 | Battery: 4 AA, ~6 months

If you have an iPhone or Apple Watch, the Schlage Encode Plus is a game-changer. It supports Apple Home Key, which means you literally tap your device to the lock to open it — no app, no fingerprint, even if your phone battery is dead (thanks to power reserve). The lock itself is built like a fortress: solid zinc construction, a keyed cylinder for backup, and Schlage’s proven keyway that resists bumping.

I’ve used this lock for four months now. The auto-lock feature gives me a notification every time the door closes, and I can set unique codes for the dog walker that expire after an hour. My only gripe is the price, but when I compare it to a full security system, it’s a bargain.

Pros: Home Key support, Grade 1 security, works with Alexa/Google, built-in Wi-Fi (no hub).
Cons: Expensive, battery life could be better, Android users miss out on tap-to-unlock.

2. Best Budget: Wyze Lock Bolt (Bluetooth)#

Price: ~$70 | Grade: Not officially rated (but feels Grade 2) | Battery: 4 AA, ~12 months

Don’t let the price fool you. The Wyze Lock Bolt is a keypad-only deadbolt replacement that skips the physical keyhole entirely — which is actually a security plus, since there’s no cylinder to pick. The keypad is durable and backlit, and you can set up to 50 unique codes. It uses Bluetooth and isn’t Wi-Fi native, but you can buy a $15 hub to enable remote access.

I put this on my garage entry door six months ago. It has survived Michigan weather, direct rain, and kids slamming it shut without a hiccup. The app is straightforward: you get logs of who unlocked the door and when. The only downside is that without the hub, you can’t control it away from home, but for a budget lock, that’s acceptable.

Pros: Punchy price, no cylinder to pick, generous code sharing, long battery life.
Cons: No physical key, requires hub for remote access, no advanced smart home triggers.

3. Best for Renters: August Wi-Fi Smart Lock (4th Gen)#

Price: ~$230 | Grade: Works with existing deadbolt (retains original security) | Battery: 2 CR123, ~3 months

As a renter, you can’t always replace your deadbolt. The August lock attaches to the back of your existing deadbolt, leaving the exterior unchanged. Your landlord’s key still works, but you get keyless entry, auto-unlock when you approach, and full Wi-Fi control built in. It’s also tiny — about the size of a doorknob.

I used this in my last apartment. Installation took five minutes with a screwdriver. The auto-unlock feature uses your phone’s location so the door opens as you walk up, which feels magical. I gave my virtual keys to friends when I traveled, and I could see every time the door was unlocked. Just note: the CR123 batteries aren’t as common as AAs, so keep spares.

Pros: No permanent changes, easy install, built-in Wi-Fi, works with Alexa/Google/HomeKit.
Cons: Batteries don’t last super long, pricey for a retrofit, doesn’t improve the physical lock itself.

4. Best with Fingerprint: Ultraloq U-Bolt Pro#

Price: ~$200 | Grade: Not rated (but solid zinc) | Battery: 4 AA, ~8 months

If you want the James Bond experience, the U-Bolt Pro opens six ways: fingerprint, code, smartphone app, physical key, shake-your-phone, and even an Apple Watch app. The fingerprint sensor is fast and stores up to 20 prints. It’s perfect for families — no one needs to remember a code or carry a key.

I installed this on my front door, and my kids (7 and 10) love using their fingers to get in. The lock is built tough: zinc alloy construction, an anti-peep keypad (you can type random digits before/after your code to confuse onlookers), and a backup keyway. The app isn’t as polished as Schlage’s, but the hardware makes up for it.

Pros: Fingerprint access, anti-peep keypad, works offline, versatile unlocking methods.
Cons: App feels dated, Bluetooth-only without a $50 hub, bulkier design.

5. Best for Google Home Users: Yale Assure Lock 2 (Zigbee/Thread)#

Price: ~$250 (with plug-in module) | Grade: ANSI 2 | Battery: 4 AA, ~12 months

The Yale Assure Lock 2 is a modular system: you choose a wireless module (Zigbee, Z-Wave, or Matter/Thread) that slides into the top. For Google Home users who want reliable, local control without cloud dependency, the Thread module is fantastic. The lock itself has no keyhole — just a touchscreen keypad — which eliminates lock bumping entirely.

I tested the Zigbee version with a SmartThings hub for three weeks. The touchscreen wakes instantly, and you can set one-time codes from your phone. The silent operation is a nice touch if you’re coming home late and don’t want to wake anyone. If the battery dies, you can press a 9V battery to the bottom terminals to power it up temporarily.

Pros: Modular for future upgrades, reliable local smart home control, sleek look, excellent battery.
Cons: No physical key (some see this as a pro), touchscreen can be finicky in rain, not the cheapest.

Smart Lock Installation Tips#

I’ve installed enough of these to know where the pain points are. Here’s what I wish I’d known on my first attempt.

DIY vs Professional Install#

Most smart locks replace your existing deadbolt and use the same holes. If your door is standard (1-3/4" to 2-1/4" thick, with 2-1/8" bore hole), you can install one in about 20 minutes with a Phillips screwdriver. The instructions are clear, and many manufacturers have video walkthroughs.

If your door is older or misaligned, you may need a chisel to widen the strike plate area. That’s when you might call a locksmith — but 90% of people can handle it themselves. Pro tip: before you remove your old lock, open the door and take a photo of the latch and strike plate positions. It’ll save you a headache when aligning the new one.

A Simple Alignment Trick#

If your new smart lock motor struggles to turn the deadbolt, the door alignment is off. Instead of moving the whole lock, try adjusting the strike plate on the door frame. Loosen the screws, shift it slightly, and tighten while testing the lock. Sometimes a tiny 1mm adjustment makes the difference between a grinding motor and a smooth lock.

Smart Lock Safety Features You Can’t Ignore#

A smart lock is only as secure as the habits around it. Here are a few features that matter more than you think.

  • Auto-lock timer: Set it to lock 30 seconds after the door closes. On models like the Wyze Lock Bolt, this is a lifesaver if you have kids who forget.
  • Entry logs: Check the app periodically to see who opened the door and when. On my August lock, I noticed the dog walker was arriving 15 minutes late — not a security breach, but helpful info.
  • Tamper alerts: Some locks will blare a siren or send a push notification if someone tries to force the keypad or remove the cover. The Schlage Encode Plus does this well.
  • Remote access: Being able to lock/unlock from anywhere isn’t just convenient; it means you can let a trusted neighbor in during an emergency without hiding a key under the mat.

Avoid “Geofencing Only” Unlocks#

Some locks offer to unlock automatically when your phone approaches. It’s convenient, but if someone steals your phone, they’re at your door. I recommend using geofencing only with two-factor authentication or combining it with a PIN/code requirement on the phone itself.

Final Thoughts#

A good smart lock won’t turn your home into Fort Knox, but it will remove the most common security flaw: human forgetfulness. After months of testing, the Schlage Encode Plus is my top pick for its build quality and Home Key integration, but the Wyze Lock Bolt offers incredible value for less than $100. The best lock for you is the one you’ll actually use — and one that fits your door, your smart home ecosystem, and your budget.

Whichever you choose, take 20 minutes to install it properly, enable auto-lock, and educate everyone in the house on how to use it. When you can close your eyes at night and know your door is locked without getting out of bed, you’ll wonder why you didn’t upgrade sooner.
